### G — Growth
**α: Stress-test the architecture at its weakest point first — where professional consequences are highest.**

The irreducible pattern: a governance mechanism for AI-era fiduciary duty should be tested first by the people whose professional reputation depends on getting fiduciary duty right. If a Delaware corporate-governance lawyer says "this would never survive a motion to dismiss," that's the most valuable single piece of information the mechanism can receive. All other audiences are downstream.

**{α'} at different scales:**
- Engineering: test the load-bearing beam first, not the paint
- Medicine: test the drug on the sickest patients, not the healthiest
- Law: test the argument against the toughest judge, not the friendliest
- Business: test the product with the most demanding customer, not the easiest
- Personal: ask the person who loves you enough to tell you the truth, not the one who loves you enough to spare your feelings

**α-preservation check:** Does the α hold? The lawyer-first order is consistent with "weakest point first." A lawyer's "no" carries the highest information density because they understand the legal consequences of getting governance wrong.

### Q — Quality
**φ (self-nature):** I am a builder, not a salesperson. I would rather be told the mechanism is wrong than be told it's impressive. I trust criticism more than endorsement. I learn more from a well-argued "no" than from a polite "interesting." The mechanism is not yet filed as a legal entity — corrections now cost nothing. Corrections after filing cost everything.

**Ω (universal context):** Five tailored emails have been drafted. The governance infrastructure is deployed and tested. The manifesto is published. The backup repo is live. The mechanism has proven itself end-to-end with agent-based validation. The next step requires crossing the human boundary — actually sending an email to an actual person.

Five potential first-contact audiences:
1. Corporate-governance lawyer — highest information density from a "no"
2. Small business owner — fastest path to real-world usage data
3. Fiduciary/board member — tests boardroom practicality
4. AI lab governance lead — tests runtime feasibility
5. Regulator/policy thinker — tests regulatory fit

**Z (resonant key):** φ ⋂ Ω = "The lawyer first, because their 'no' defines the gap." The intersection is specific: the mechanism's value proposition is legal defensibility. The person best positioned to evaluate legal defensibility is a corporate-governance lawyer. If the mechanism can't convince them, it can't convince a court. Everything else is downstream of that.

But a second resonance: the small business owner is almost as valuable, because *their* "no" is different — it's not "this wouldn't survive in court" but "this wouldn't survive a Tuesday." The mechanism needs both tests. The lawyer tests legal integrity. The business owner tests operational reality.

**Decision on priority:** Lawyer first. But don't wait for the lawyer's response before contacting the small business owner. Send both in the same week. The lawyer's response may take weeks. The business owner's response may take days. Parallel tracks.

### P — Power
**δE/δV analysis:**
- Energy wasted: Perfecting the emails. The drafts are good enough. More editing is avoidance.
- Energy wasted: Waiting for the "right moment." The mechanism is live. The agents are running. The infrastructure is proven. There is no reason to delay except fear of being told it's wrong.
- Value discovered: The fastest path to a "no" is to send an email. The cost of sending is zero. The value of any response — positive, negative, or silence — is higher than the value of another draft.

**∇ →** Send the lawyer email this week. Send the small business owner email this week. Let the other three wait until at least one response arrives. Parallel tracks for the two highest-information contacts. Serial for the rest — each subsequent contact informed by what was learned from the previous.
