# First Contact — Thinking Partner Outreach

## The Strategy

Don't broadcast. Don't launch. Send five emails to five specific people. Each email is tailored to their world — not a template with the name swapped. Each asks a question they're uniquely positioned to answer.

The goal is not to convince. The goal is to learn where the architecture breaks when it meets real professional judgment.

---

## 1. Corporate-Governance Lawyer

**Profile:** Partner at a Delaware corporate firm. Has handled derivative litigation. Understands *Caremark/Marchand/McDonald's* not as citations but as cases they've briefed.

**Subject:** A fiduciary duty for the AI era — would you tell a client to look at this?

**Body:**

> [Name],
>
> I've been working on a structural answer to a problem I think you've already seen: AI systems now draft the resolutions boards vote on, model the strategic alternatives they compare, and generate the risk language in committee reports. The record can't show where independent human judgment was exercised at the threshold of decision.
>
> The lawyer-fabrication cases (*Mata v. Avianca*, *Johnson v. Dunn*, 1,300+ tracked filings) are the early warning. The boardroom version is coming.
>
> I've drafted a governance mechanism — a constitutional grammar — that includes a new fiduciary duty: the Duty of Membrane Integrity. It's a Bylaws-level obligation to preserve the structural boundary between human judgment and AI-assisted input in every material decision. Verifiable in part by a deterministic checker. Examinable by a court in 2050 against artifacts sealed today.
>
> The instruments are published openly at [5qln.com](https://www.5qln.com). They include a Certificate of Incorporation, paired Bylaws (Human + AI OS Editions, hash-matched), and a Final Blueprint with 35 architectural rows.
>
> I'm not asking you to endorse this. I'm asking: what would have to change before you would advise a client to look at it? If the answer is "it would never be ready," that's useful. If the answer is "change sections X, Y, Z and it becomes interesting," that's more useful.
>
> Would you have 20 minutes to tell me where it's wrong?
>
> Amihai

**Why this person first:** A corporate-governance lawyer's "no" is the most informative possible response. If the mechanism can't survive contact with someone who lives in *Caremark*, it's not ready for anyone else.

## 3. Small Business Owner (Sole Proprietor or Partnership)

**Profile:** Someone running a real business — consultancy, agency, professional services. Uses AI daily. Has liability exposure. Doesn't have a board.

**Subject:** I tested a governance mechanism on my own decisions — two ruptures, three intact

**Body:**

> [Name],
>
> I've been building a simple governance mechanism for people who use AI in their business and want a record of where they thought vs. where the AI generated. It's not compliance software. It's a decision record that takes about 10 minutes to fill out.
>
> I stress-tested it by modeling five real decisions from a solo design consultancy. Two failed — once because the designer asked AI for an answer instead of a question, once because she brought anger masquerading as inquiry. Three held — including an existential pivot and a first hire.
>
> The finding: the mechanism works for a sole proprietor, but only if she's willing to produce honest records. The grammar catches structural failures. It can't catch a lie.
>
> I'm looking for someone who actually runs a small business and uses AI in decision-making — to try producing three decision records over one week and tell me whether the overhead is worth the trail it leaves.
>
> Would you be willing to test this in your actual business for one week? I'll send you the template — it's one page.
>
> Amihai

**Why this person:** The small business adapter is theoretical until a real small business uses it. A sole proprietor or small partner who tries it for a week and says "this was 10 minutes well spent" or "this was theater" — either answer is vastly more valuable than another document.

## 4. Regulator / Policy Thinker

**Profile:** Someone who works on AI governance policy — at NIST, an EU institution, a national AI safety institute, or a policy think tank. Understands that regulation governs what organizations report, not what happens at the threshold of decision.

**Subject:** The gap between NIST AI RMF and the moment a director signs

**Body:**

> [Name],
>
> Existing AI governance frameworks — NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, the EU AI Act — all describe what organizations should do *around* their AI systems. None of them produce a structurally verifiable record of where, in any specific decision, the substantive judgment formed.
>
> Regulation governs what organizations report. It does not govern what happens at the moment a human director either exercises judgment or ratifies output.
>
> I've built a voluntary fiduciary architecture that operates at that moment — the threshold of decision. It's a constitutional grammar (217 bytes, open-source) that produces decision records showing exactly where AI contributed and where human judgment formed. Verifiable in part by a deterministic checker that needs no AI to run. Examinable by a court in 2050 against artifacts sealed today.
>
> I'm not arguing this should be mandatory. I'm asking: what relationship between this voluntary architecture and existing positive law would make it complementary rather than redundant? If a company adopted this voluntarily, would it satisfy any existing regulatory requirements? Would it create any new ones?
>
> The full specification is at [5qln.com](https://www.5qln.com). The governance instruments are at [github.com/qlnlife/Governancesmallbiz](https://github.com/qlnlife/Governancesmallbiz).
>
> Would you have time to discuss where voluntary governance infrastructure fits in the regulatory landscape?
>
> Amihai

## 5. Fiduciary (Board Member / Director)

**Profile:** Someone who actually serves on a board. Has signed resolutions. Has relied on management presentations. Has wondered — at least once — "how much of this deck did an AI write, and does it matter?"

**Subject:** When you sign a board resolution, do you know where the draft came from?

**Body:**

> [Name],
>
> Here's a question I suspect you've already asked yourself in some form: when you vote on a board resolution, do you know which parts were drafted by a human and which by a generative model?
>
> Current board minutes record what was decided. They don't record how the decision formed. AI doesn't need to vote to shape the outcome — it just needs to draft the resolution, model the alternatives, and generate the risk language. The director signs. Two years later, if something goes wrong, the record can't show where independent judgment was exercised.
>
> I've been working on a governance mechanism that addresses this directly — a Duty of Membrane Integrity that requires the boundary between human judgment and AI input to be visible and recorded in every material decision. It's not a policy. It's a fiduciary duty, of the same family as the duties recognized in *Caremark*.
>
> The question I have for you is purely practical: if this mechanism existed in your boardroom — a 10-minute decision record per material decision — would it help or get in the way of your actual practice? What would it need to look like for you to actually use it?
>
> I'm not selling anything. I'm stress-testing an architecture. Your honest answer — especially if it's "this would never work in a real boardroom, and here's why" — is exactly what I need.
>
> Amihai

## Priority Order

1. **Corporate-governance lawyer first.** Their "no" defines the gap.
2. **Small business owner second.** Their "I tried it" is the proof.
3. **Fiduciary third.** Their "here's what would actually happen in a boardroom" is the reality check.
4. **AI lab lead fourth.** Their "here's what's impossible at runtime" refines the AI OS Edition.
5. **Regulator fifth.** Their "here's where it fits in the landscape" opens institutional adoption.

---

## What Success Looks Like

Not five yeses. Five conversations. Each one either:
- Identifies a flaw we can fix → update the mechanism
- Identifies a flaw we can't fix → update the claim
- Confirms the mechanism holds under professional scrutiny → advance to the next ring

The master page asked for thinking partners. This is how you find them.
