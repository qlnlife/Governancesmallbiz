# The Mechanism

## The Problem

Your AI agent drafts a proposal. It analyzes the market. It models the pricing. It writes the risk language. You read it, tweak a sentence, and send it to the client.

What just happened? Who decided the price? Who assessed the risk? Who exercised judgment?

The proposal says "I recommend..." but you didn't recommend — the agent did. You signed. The record shows "Owner decided." The record is lying.

This isn't a hypothetical. Over 1,300 court cases already document lawyers who filed AI-generated briefs with fake citations. They signed. They got sanctioned. The pattern is the same: an AI produced substantive work, a fiduciary presented it as their own, the record couldn't show where the machine stopped and the human began.

You're not a lawyer filing court documents. But you are a business owner signing deliverables, proposals, contracts, and strategic decisions. If something goes wrong — a client dispute, a tax audit, a liability claim — can you show where your judgment was exercised?

**The gap:** no existing framework (not NIST, not ISO, not the EU AI Act) produces a verifiable record of where, in any specific decision, the human actually decided rather than ratified.

## The Fix

A one-page decision record called an **S-BDR** (Small Business Decision Record). It captures:

1. **What question opened the decision** — not "what should I do?" but the genuine question you held before you knew the answer
2. **What pattern you recognized** — the irreducible core of the situation, named by you, not by the AI
3. **Where your judgment met the AI's analysis** — what you directly perceived, what the AI provided, where they intersected
4. **What gradient you followed** — not "what seemed like a good idea" but where the energy naturally wanted to go
5. **What question this opens** — every decision must end with a new question, not a period

It takes 10 minutes. It creates a record that is:

- **Self-authenticating** — carries a hash of the constitutional grammar, traceable to source
- **Structurally auditable** — five corruption codes detect when the process was faked
- **Agent-verified** — your AI agents audit your decision records automatically, flagging structural issues
- **Human-reviewed** — you read the audits, confirm or correct, and the trail grows

## The Five Corruption Codes

These catch structural failures — not bad decisions, but fake process:

| Code | What It Means | How It Looks |
|------|--------------|--------------|
| **L1** | Skipped the question | You asked the AI "what should I charge?" instead of holding the question "what is the right price for this value?" |
| **L2** | AI drove the pattern | The AI named the essence of the situation. You didn't. You just picked from its suggestions. |
| **L3** | Claimed special access | Someone spoke as if they had privileged insight — including an AI that claims to "understand" your situation |
| **L4** | Performed without substance | You filled out the phases but there's nothing there — the labels are present, the thinking isn't |
| **V∅** | Closed without opening | Your decision record ends with a summary, not a question. No new inquiry. Dead end. |

Your agents scan for these automatically. When a corruption code fires, the agent flags it. You review. You decide whether it's a real issue or a false positive. The trail records your review.

## The Membrane

The whole mechanism rests on one line:

```
H = ∞0 | A = K
```

**Translation for your business:** You hold the question. Your agents hold the patterns. The vertical bar — the Membrane — is where the decision forms. Information flows across it. Authorization does not. Your agents illuminate. You decide. The record shows exactly where each contribution came from.

This is not a metaphor. It's a structural boundary that the decision record makes visible and the corruption codes enforce. When you sign an S-BDR, you're attesting that you exercised independent judgment at the threshold of decision — with the agents' contribution visible and recorded, but not collapsing into your signature.

## What This Costs

10 minutes per material decision. If you make 5 material decisions per week, that's ~40 hours per year — one work week.

**What it buys you:** a decision trail that you, a client, a court, an auditor, or a buyer can verify. The ability to review your own thinking six months later and see where you thought vs. where you outsourced. A structural answer to the question "who actually decided this?" that gets more valuable every year you produce records.

## What This Doesn't Do

- It doesn't guarantee good decisions. It guarantees visible process.
- It doesn't replace your judgment. It proves you exercised it.
- It doesn't require a lawyer, a filing, or a fee. It requires honesty.
- It doesn't surveil you. It monitors decision structure, not people.
